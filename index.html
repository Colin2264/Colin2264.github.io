Overview

This project involves the full-stack design and development of a two-wheeled self-balancing robot — from mathematical modeling through hardware integration. The goal is a robot that maintains stable upright balance in real time, responding dynamically to disturbances and surface changes.

Control System Design

A Linear Quadratic Regulator (LQR) and PID control were used to control the robot and the performance was compared. The mathematical model was derived from first principles — linearizing the inverted pendulum equations of motion around the upright equilibrium point.

Stability simulations were performed in MATLAB and Simulink to tune the LQR weighting matrices and validate closed-loop response before committing to hardware. Step response, disturbance rejection, and robustness margins were all verified in simulation.

Hardware & Fabrication

The mechanical frame was designed in SolidWorks and fabricated using additive manufacturing via Bambu Studio. Sensor fusion combines IMU accelerometer and gyroscope data using a complementary filter to produce a reliable tilt angle estimate. Motor drivers and encoders close the velocity feedback loop on the wheel controllers.

Control algorithms are implemented in C++ on Arduino IDE, running with hard real-time loop timing to ensure deterministic control execution.

Current Status

Simulation and controller evaluation completed. Comparative testing between PID and LQR control methods showed a marginal performance improvement with the LQR controller, primarily in stability and response smoothness. The limited difference in performance is likely due to the relatively simple dynamics of the system, where a well-tuned PID controller can already achieve near-optimal stabilization under nominal operating conditions.

Overview

The Pendubot is a double-pendulum robotic system consisting of two rigid links connected in series, with actuation only at the first joint. The challenge is to swing the system up from the stable hanging position and balance it in the unstable inverted configuration — a classic underactuated control problem that requires coordinating two links using only one motor.

Modeling

The system was modeled in SolidWorks and the dynamics were derived from the equations of motion for a double pendulum. The nonlinear model captures the full coupled dynamics of both links, accounting for inertia, gravity, and the kinematic constraints of the underactuated joint. This model serves as the foundation for both simulation and controller design.

Control Strategy

Two distinct controllers are used in sequence. An energy-based swing-up controller pumps energy into the system to drive it toward the inverted equilibrium — modulating the torque at the actuated joint to increase total mechanical energy toward the target level without requiring a linearized model.

Once the system approaches the inverted position, control is handed off to a Linear Quadratic Regulator (LQR), which stabilizes both links in the upright configuration. The LQR is designed from the linearized dynamics around the unstable equilibrium, with weighting matrices tuned to balance disturbance rejection against actuator effort.

Current Status

Modeling and simulation are currently underway in MATLAB. Hardware design and fabrication will follow once the control strategy is validated in simulation. Photos and video will be added as the project progresses.

Overview

Many people have difficulty maintaining focus during everyday tasks at work or school. A common response is to fidget — drumming fingers, clicking a pen, cracking knuckles. The Vibrotactile Fidget Band is a wrist-worn device that mimics these natural fidgeting behaviors through programmable vibration patterns, redirecting the need for stimulation away from the hands so users can remain fully engaged in their tasks.

Existing solutions either occupy the hands, require active attention, or limit the user to a single-use tool. The wrist was chosen specifically because it can deliver haptic stimuli without encumbering the hands — making the device compatible with typing, writing, or any dual-handed task — while blending naturally into professional environments.

Hardware Design

The device is built around an Arduino Nano microcontroller running custom vibration pattern firmware. Five flat coin-type ERM vibration motors are distributed across the wristband — two on the dorsal side and three on the palmar side — corresponding intuitively to each finger. The palmar side carries an additional motor to take advantage of its superior tactile acuity.

All components are housed in a 3D-printed wristband casing and powered by a 3.7V lithium-ion battery, keeping the total build cost within a $50 budget. Future iterations will replace the ERM motors with linear resonant actuators (LRAs) for sharper, lower-latency haptic pulses, and will use a custom PCB to reduce overall size.

Experiment & Methodology

Ten participants completed the PEBL Continuous Performance Test (CPT) — a 14-minute task designed to induce mind-wandering and fidgeting — both with and without the device. A camera recorded each session to allow post-hoc quantification of fidgeting frequency. Three hypotheses were tested: improved task accuracy and reaction time (H1), reduced fidgeting frequency (H2), and user preference for the device (H3). A Repeated Measures ANOVA was used to evaluate statistical significance.

Results

The key finding was a statistically significant reduction in fidgeting frequency across all ten participants when the device was worn (ANOVA p = 0.003). One participant went from 43 observed fidgeting events to just 6 during the same 14-minute test. User preference was strongly positive: 8 of 10 preferred the device and said they would purchase it if commercially available. All three participants with diagnosed ADHD reported feeling noticeably calmer while wearing the device.

Future Work

Future iterations will focus on reducing device size via a custom PCB, switching to LRA actuators for crisper haptic feedback, and adding wireless operation with a smartphone app for toggling patterns and adjusting intensity. Longer-term applications include anxiety reduction, sleep improvement, and closed-loop systems that detect mind-wandering in real time.
